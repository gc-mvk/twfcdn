Introduction and Etymological Origin

Kaaba (Arabic: الكعبة), is the most sacred cubic structure in Islam, located at the center of Masjid al-Haram in the city of Mecca, Saudi Arabia. The word is derived from the root "ka'b" (cube or heel).

Muslims, wherever they are in the world, turn towards this structure when performing their prayers. This direction is called the Qibla. The Kaaba is also the center of tawaf, the most important part of the Hajj and Umrah rituals.

Among the other names of the Kaaba mentioned in the Quran are Baytullah (House of Allah) and al-Bayt al-Atiq (The Ancient House). These names emphasize both that the ownership of the structure symbolically belongs to Allah and its historical antiquity.

History: From First Construction to Today

According to Islamic belief, the foundations of the Kaaba were first laid by Prophet Adam. However, over time this structure disappeared due to natural events and neglect. Later, by the command of Allah, it was rebuilt by Prophet Abraham and his son Prophet Ishmael by rediscovering its original foundations.

The Kaaba has endured many hardships throughout history. The structure, which was filled with 360 idols inside and around it during the Age of Ignorance, was restored to its original state and completely purified from idols after Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) conquered Mecca (in 630 AD).

The structure has been damaged many times due to floods, fires, and sieges. Major restorations were carried out during the Umayyad, Abbasid, and Ottoman periods. Especially during the reign of Ottoman Sultan Murad IV (1630), after a major flood, the Kaaba was almost completely rebuilt and reached its current solid form.

Masjid al-Haram is the large courtyard surrounding the Kaaba. It has been expanded over centuries by caliphs and sultans. With massive expansion projects carried out during the Saudi Arabian period, today millions of Muslims can worship simultaneously.


Architectural Elements and Sections

The height of the Kaaba is approximately 13.1 meters. The lengths of its sides are not exactly equal, indicating that the structure is not a perfect cube but a rectangular prism close to a cube. The corners of the structure are positioned to face geographical directions.

Hajar al-Aswad: The black stone located on the eastern corner of the Kaaba, about 1.5 meters above the ground. It marks the starting point of tawaf. According to belief, this stone came from heaven and turned black due to human sins.

Maqam Ibrahim: Located a few meters from the Kaaba, it is the stone upon which Prophet Abraham stood during construction and is believed to bear his footprint. This area is a sacred place where the tawaf prayer is performed.

Hijr Ismail (Hatim): The semi-circular area in front of the northwestern wall of the Kaaba, enclosed by a low wall. Since it is considered part of the Kaaba, praying here is like praying inside the structure.

Multazam: The wall between Hajar al-Aswad and the door of the Kaaba. It is known as one of the most accepted places for prayers. The Golden Spout channels rainwater from the roof into Hijr Ismail.

The covering of the Kaaba is called the Kiswah. It is woven from pure silk and embroidered with verses using gold and silver threads. This covering is replaced every year on the morning of Eid al-Adha.

Spiritual Value

The Kaaba is not just a stone structure for Muslims; it is the greatest symbol on earth of monotheism, unity, and submission to Allah.
